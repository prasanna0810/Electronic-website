Practice of Soldering and De-soldering

Listen to Audio Explanation

1. Introduction

Soldering and desoldering are essential techniques in electronics and electrical engineering. Soldering is the process of joining two or more metal surfaces using a filler material, known as solder, which has a lower melting point than the materials being joined. It is used in PCB (Printed Circuit Board) assembly, component repairs, and various electrical connections. Desoldering is the reverse process, used to remove solder and separate components for repair or replacement. Mastery of these techniques is crucial for assembling, maintaining, and troubleshooting electronic circuits.

What Makes a Good Solder Joint?

A proper solder joint should have these characteristics:

  • Smooth, shiny surface (not dull or grainy)
  • Concave shape that forms a fillet between the component and pad
  • Complete wetting of both surfaces
  • No excess solder or bridges to adjacent connections
  • No visible cracks or holes

2. Tools and Materials Required

2.1 Soldering Tools and Equipment

Soldering Iron

A heated tool that melts the solder. It comes in various wattages (15W-60W) depending on the application. A lower wattage iron is suitable for delicate electronics, while higher wattage is used for heavier components.

Soldering Station

A temperature-controlled soldering iron with adjustable settings, preferred for professional work to prevent overheating and component damage.

Solder

A metal alloy (commonly tin-lead or lead-free) used to create strong electrical connections. Common compositions include 60/40 (tin/lead) and lead-free varieties.

Flux

A chemical agent that removes oxidation, cleans metal surfaces, and helps solder flow properly. Available in paste, liquid, and core formats.

Tip Cleaner

Sponge or brass tip cleaner used to clean the soldering iron tip, ensuring proper heat transfer and preventing oxidation.

Helping Hands

A stand with alligator clips to hold components steady during soldering, often including a magnifying glass for precision work.

Desoldering Braid

A copper mesh that absorbs melted solder for easy removal, also known as solder wick.

Solder Sucker

A vacuum tool used to extract molten solder quickly, also called a desoldering pump.

Hot Air Rework Station

Used for desoldering surface-mount components, providing controlled heat for removal without damaging the PCB.

Magnifying Glass

Helps in inspecting fine solder joints, especially in microelectronics and surface-mount work.

Types of Solder
  • 60/40 (60% tin, 40% lead): Lower melting point (183-190¬∞C), smooth flow, creates reliable joints.
  • 63/37 (63% tin, 37% lead): Eutectic solder that transitions directly from solid to liquid, reducing "cold joints".
  • Lead-free solder (Sn-Ag-Cu): Environmentally friendly but requires higher temperature (217-220¬∞C).
  • Flux-core solder: Contains flux in the center, eliminating the need for separate flux application in most cases.

3. Soldering Process

3.1 Preparing the Work Area

1
Ventilation

Ensure the workspace is clean, well-lit, and well-ventilated to avoid inhaling fumes.

2
Safety Equipment

Wear safety goggles and heat-resistant gloves to prevent burns and exposure to hazardous materials.

3
Work Surface

Keep a fire-resistant mat or surface for soldering to prevent accidental burns or fire hazards.

4
Iron Setup

Plug in the soldering iron and set the recommended temperature (350¬∞C-400¬∞C for most applications, 250¬∞C-300¬∞C for delicate SMD work).

5
Component Preparation

Secure components using a PCB holder or third-hand tool to ensure stability during soldering.

3.2 Preparing the Components

1
Clean the Metal Surfaces

Use isopropyl alcohol or flux to remove dirt, grease, and oxidation from component leads and PCB pads.

2
Apply Flux

This enhances solder adhesion and prevents oxidation during the soldering process.

3
Pre-Tin the Leads

Apply a small amount of solder to component leads before placement for better joint formation.

3.3 Soldering Steps

1
Tinning the Iron Tip

Melt a small amount of solder onto the iron tip to improve heat transfer and prevent oxidation.

2
Heating the Joint

Place the iron tip on both the component lead and PCB pad simultaneously for even heat distribution.

3
Applying Solder

Touch the solder wire to the heated joint (not the iron tip) and let it flow naturally into the connection.

4
Removing the Solder and Iron

Once the joint is formed, remove the solder wire first, then the iron.

5
Cooling and Inspection

Allow the joint to cool naturally. A good joint should be shiny, smooth, and concave in shape.

3.4 Common Soldering Issues and Fixes

Issue Cause Solution
Cold Joint Insufficient heat or movement during cooling Reheat the joint and apply fresh solder with flux
Excess Solder Too much solder applied Use a desoldering pump or braid to remove excess
Bridging Solder connecting two adjacent joints Remove excess solder using wick or pump
Dull Joints Oxidation or incorrect solder type Use fresh solder and clean surfaces
Solder Balling Inconsistent heating or contaminated surfaces Use proper flux and heat control
Soldering Best Practices
  • Keep the iron tip clean by wiping it frequently on a damp sponge or brass cleaner.
  • Apply heat to both surfaces being joined, not just one side.
  • Use the minimum amount of solder needed for a reliable connection.
  • Hold components steady until the solder has completely solidified.
  • Maintain the iron tip by regularly tinning it with fresh solder.
  • For temperature-sensitive components, use heat sinks to prevent damage.

4. Desoldering Techniques

Desoldering is required for repairing circuits or replacing faulty components. There are multiple methods:

4.1 Using a Desoldering Pump (Solder Sucker)

1
Prepare the Pump

Press down the plunger to create vacuum potential.

2
Heat the Joint

Heat the solder joint using the soldering iron until it becomes completely molten.

3
Position and Activate

Position the desoldering pump close to the molten solder and press the release button to suck up the solder.

4
Repeat if Necessary

Repeat the process as necessary to clear the joint completely.

4.2 Using Desoldering Braid (Wick)

1
Position the Braid

Place the wick over the solder joint you want to remove.

2
Apply Heat

Heat the braid with the soldering iron until the solder melts and is absorbed into the copper mesh.

3
Remove and Trim

Remove the braid carefully and cut off the used portion as it becomes saturated with solder.

4
Clean Residue

Clean any remaining residue with isopropyl alcohol.

4.3 Using a Hot Air Rework Station (For SMD Components)

1
Set Temperature

Set the hot air gun to the appropriate temperature (typically 250¬∞C-300¬∞C) and airflow rate.

2
Apply Heat

Direct the airflow evenly over the component, moving in small circles to distribute heat uniformly.

3
Remove Component

Once the solder melts, carefully remove the component using tweezers.

4
Clean PCB Pads

Clean the PCB pads using solder wick to prepare for the new component.

Desoldering Precautions
  • Avoid applying excessive heat that could damage the PCB or nearby components.
  • Be especially careful with multi-layer PCBs as excessive heat can delaminate the layers.
  • For sensitive components, use heat sinks to protect adjacent parts.
  • When using hot air, be mindful of small components that could be blown away.
  • Always wear appropriate eye protection as solder can occasionally splatter.

5. Safety Precautions

Essential Safety Guidelines
  • Work in a ventilated area: Use an exhaust fan or fume extractor to avoid inhaling toxic fumes.
  • Avoid direct contact with the iron tip: It can reach temperatures of 400¬∞C and cause severe burns.
  • Handle lead-based solder with care: Wash hands after use and avoid food consumption while soldering.
  • Keep flammable materials away: Soldering involves high temperatures that can ignite nearby objects.
  • Use proper eye protection: Prevent accidental splashes of molten solder or flux from entering the eyes.
  • Store tools properly: Always unplug and safely store the soldering iron when not in use.
First Aid for Soldering Burns
  1. Cool the burn immediately with cool (not cold) running water for at least 10-15 minutes.
  2. Do not apply ice directly to the burn as it can cause further tissue damage.
  3. Do not apply creams, ointments, or butter to the burn.
  4. Cover the burn with a sterile, non-adhesive bandage or clean cloth.
  5. Seek medical attention for severe burns or burns that affect large areas.

6. Advanced Soldering Techniques

Reflow Soldering

Used for surface-mount technology (SMT) components, where solder paste is pre-applied and then heated in an oven or with a controlled heat source to create multiple joints simultaneously.

1
Apply Solder Paste

Use a stencil to apply solder paste precisely to the PCB pads.

2
Place Components

Position SMD components accurately on the pads with tweezers or a pick-and-place machine.

3
Heat Application

Apply controlled heat following a specific temperature profile (preheat, soak, reflow, and cooling).

4
Cooling

Allow the PCB to cool gradually to prevent thermal stress on components and joints.

Micro Soldering

Specialized technique for repairing tiny components, often used in mobile phone and laptop repairs.

Micro Soldering Requirements
  • High-quality microscope or magnifying system (10-30x magnification)
  • Fine-tipped soldering iron (0.1-0.5mm tips)
  • Precision tweezers and micro-tools
  • Thin solder wire (0.3-0.5mm diameter)
  • Specialized flux formulated for micro-electronics
  • Steady hands and patience

BGA Rework

Advanced soldering method used for Ball Grid Array (BGA) chips requiring precise reflow temperatures.

1
Component Removal

Use specialized BGA rework station to precisely heat and remove the existing component.

2
Pad Cleaning

Clean the PCB pads thoroughly and apply fresh flux.

3
Ball Replacement

Apply new solder balls to the BGA component or PCB pads using a stencil.

4
Precise Alignment

Position the new component with exact alignment using optical systems.

5
Controlled Reflow

Apply precise heat following a specific temperature profile to form reliable connections.

Drag Soldering

A technique for soldering multiple pins quickly using a flat-tipped iron and flux.

1
Apply Flux

Apply generous flux to all pins that need soldering.

2
Load Solder

Apply a small amount of solder to the iron tip.

3
Drag Technique

Drag the iron tip across all pins in one smooth motion, allowing surface tension to distribute solder.

4
Clean Bridges

Use solder wick to remove any bridges that may have formed between pins.

7. Conclusion

Soldering and desoldering are vital skills in electronics. Proper technique ensures reliable connections, while troubleshooting methods help maintain circuit integrity. With practice, efficiency and precision can be achieved in both assembling and repairing electronic components. Mastery of advanced techniques further expands the ability to work with modern miniaturized circuits.

Practice Recommendations
  • Begin with larger through-hole components before attempting surface-mount work.
  • Practice on scrap PCBs or inexpensive kits before working on valuable components.
  • Document your progress with photos to see improvement over time.
  • Join electronics communities to share experiences and learn from others.
  • Regularly maintain your tools to ensure consistent performance.
  • Experiment with different solder types and fluxes to understand their properties.
