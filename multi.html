Usage of Multimeter (Analog and Digital)

Listen to Audio Explanation

Introduction

A multimeter, also known as a volt-ohm meter (VOM), is an essential instrument used for measuring electrical parameters such as voltage, current, and resistance. It is widely used in electronics, electrical engineering, and maintenance to diagnose and troubleshoot electrical circuits. Multimeters come in two types: analog and digital.

This chapter provides an in-depth understanding of both types of multimeters, their components, and their proper usage in various applications. Additionally, it covers troubleshooting techniques and advanced applications of multimeters in electrical diagnostics.

Types of Multimeters

1. Analog Multimeter (AMM)

An analog multimeter uses a moving-coil meter with a needle to display readings on a printed scale. It operates on the principle of electromagnetic deflection, where current passing through a coil generates a magnetic field that moves the needle.

Features of Analog Multimeter:

  • Continuously varying readings: Ideal for monitoring trends in voltage or current.
  • No need for battery power (except for resistance measurement).
  • More sensitive to fluctuations than digital multimeters.
  • Requires manual range selection.
  • Provides a better representation of transient signals.

Disadvantages:

  • Prone to parallax errors due to needle alignment.
  • Less precise for low voltage and resistance measurements.
  • More fragile due to mechanical components.

2. Digital Multimeter (DMM)

A digital multimeter provides numerical readings on an LCD screen. It is more accurate and easier to use compared to an analog multimeter.

Features of Digital Multimeter:

  • High accuracy and resolution.
  • Auto-ranging capabilities in most models.
  • Can store and transmit data (in advanced models).
  • Less prone to parallax errors compared to analog multimeters.
  • Requires a battery for operation.
  • Can measure additional parameters such as capacitance, frequency, and temperature in advanced models.

Disadvantages:

  • More expensive than analog multimeters.
  • Requires battery power, making it less useful if the battery dies.
  • Slower response time for fluctuating signals.

Parts of a Multimeter

Regardless of type, multimeters share common components:

  • Display (Analog or Digital): Shows measurement readings.
  • Selection Dial (Rotary Switch): Chooses the measurement function (Voltage, Current, Resistance, etc.).
  • Probes:
    • Red Probe: Positive lead for measurement.
    • Black Probe: Negative lead (Common terminal).
  • Input Terminals:
    • COM (Common): The ground or reference point for measurements.
    • V/Ω Terminal: Used for voltage and resistance measurements.
    • mA Terminal: Used for low-current measurements.
    • 10A Terminal: Used for high-current measurements.
  • Battery and Fuse Compartment: Used for resistance measurements and overload protection in digital multimeters.
  • Auto-Hold Function (in DMMs): Allows the meter to freeze a reading for later analysis.
  • Backlight (in DMMs): Helps in low-light environments.
  • Range Selector (Manual or Auto-Range): Determines the precision level and range of the measurement.

How to Use a Multimeter

1. Measuring Voltage

Voltage measurement is essential for checking batteries, power supplies, and circuits.

Steps:

  1. Set the rotary switch to DCV (for DC voltage) or ACV (for AC voltage).
  2. Connect the black probe to COM terminal and the red probe to the V/Ω terminal.
  3. Place the probes across the component or circuit where voltage needs to be measured.
  4. Read the displayed value:
    • For analog meters, ensure correct scale selection.
    • For digital meters, the reading is directly displayed.
  5. Ensure the polarity is correct when measuring DC voltage.

Example: Measuring a 9V battery should display approximately 9V.

2. Measuring Current

Current measurement helps in verifying the flow of electricity in a circuit.

Steps:

  1. Set the dial to the appropriate A (Amperes) range.
  2. Insert the red probe into the correct terminal:
    • Use the mA terminal for currents below 200mA.
    • Use the 10A terminal for currents higher than 200mA.
  3. Break the circuit and connect the meter in series.
  4. Read the current value on the display.

Example: Checking the current draw of an LED circuit.

3. Measuring Resistance

Resistance measurement is useful for checking resistors, continuity, and wiring.

Steps:

  1. Set the rotary switch to Ω (Ohms).
  2. Insert the probes into COM and V/Ω terminals.
  3. Touch the probes across the component (ensure no power is applied to the circuit).
  4. Read the resistance value.

Example: Testing a 1kΩ resistor should display approximately 1000Ω.

4. Testing Continuity

Continuity tests ensure electrical pathways are intact.

Steps:

  1. Set the multimeter to continuity mode (beep mode).
  2. Place the probes on both ends of the circuit path.
  3. A beep sound indicates continuity; no beep means an open circuit.

Example: Checking the wiring in a household appliance.

5. Testing Diodes

Diodes are semiconductor devices that allow current flow in one direction.

Steps:

  1. Set the multimeter to diode test mode (marked with a diode symbol).
  2. Place the red probe on the anode and the black probe on the cathode.
  3. A low reading (0.5V-0.7V for silicon diodes) indicates a forward-biased diode.
  4. Reversing the probes should give a high/infinite reading.

Example: Testing a rectifier diode in a power supply circuit.

Advanced Applications

  • Testing Capacitors: Measuring capacitance in microfarads (µF).
  • Measuring Frequency: Checking AC signal frequency in hertz (Hz).
  • Temperature Measurement: Using a thermocouple with a DMM.
  • Checking Transistors: Identifying faulty transistors with hFE mode.

Safety Precautions

  • Always start with the highest range if unsure.
  • Never measure resistance in a live circuit.
  • Do not use a multimeter with damaged probes or wires.
  • Use proper protective gear when working with high voltages.
  • Follow the manufacturer's guidelines for maintenance and calibration.
  • Always check the fuse before measuring high currents.

Conclusion

Mastering the use of both analog and digital multimeters is essential for anyone working with electronics or electrical systems. Understanding the correct procedures for measuring voltage, current, resistance, continuity, and diodes ensures effective troubleshooting and safety. By following the outlined steps and precautions, users can accurately diagnose and repair electrical issues with confidence.
